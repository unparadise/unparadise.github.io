Windows Focus Sessions

Role: Senior Product Manager

Responsibilities: Feature incubation, feature design (with designers), and go to market plan.

Situation

The team owned a set of Windows inbox apps including Alarms & Clock, Calculator, Groove Music, Maps, Movies & TV, Notepad, and Voice Recorder when I joined. We were focusing on two features - Graphing Calculator and Apps on demand. While both projects were important, I felt that we, as a team, lacked a longer-term vision of what we want to do with these apps.

Actions

So I started to examine the portfolio of apps and understand who uses them and why. Many of the inbox apps were developed when the Windows Phone business existed. As a result, a lot of the core scenarios in the inbox apps are focus on mobile. With smart phone becomes more and more pervasive, especially for information worker. I asked the hard question of why would the users use these apps? I started by going through users feedback and talking to customers. I besides understanding the current user base of these apps, I also reached out to multiple research teams in Microsoft to get a better understanding of trends and other teams' priorities.

Through multiple conversations, I discovered that one of the top pain-points of Windows is distraction. Many users have told us that it's hard to focus and be productive. A normal information worker gets distracted every 3 mins. It takes one up to 25 mins to refocus after being distracted. I knew this is a problem users need help.

Interactive Search Query Formulation

Role: Senior Product Manager

Responsibilities: Feature incubation, feature design (with designers), and feature specing.

Situation

The Outlook team was pushing the enterprise desktop users to Outlook.com in 2021. One of the feedback the team heard from the enterprise customers is that search in Outlook.com is not as good as that in the desktop Outlook. I was hired to improve search on Outlook.com.

The team had a couple of clear customer asks (such as supporting advanced search) and had invested in a feature called guided-formulation. The goal of the feature is to help users to build more specific search queries. It surfaced keywords in the auto-suggest dropdown as users typed in the search box. The team had spent multiple years works on this.

Actions

After I had formed my hypothesis, I started to observe how other people search and to talk with friends how they search. Based on some preliminary research, I proposed the idea of a more interactive guided-formulation feature to my manager. He’s initial reaction was lukewarm. Seeing that I knew that I need to find more evidence. So I did three things. First, I reached out to Microsoft Research and share my hypothesis with a researcher who’s been working on email search. He confirmed my hypothesis and shared with me a study they did testing an idea similar to what I was proposing. Second, I sketched out a few comps and asked the user researcher in my team to see if he can get some feedback from the the real users. He took the concept to the enterprise customers through the Compass program and the feedback the idea receive is overwhelmingly positive. Third, I discussed the idea with the search ML PM. He was very excited because the user interaction in the new concept allows more relevant search keywords to be presented and reduces performance requirement.

Impact

With the additional data from Microsoft Research, user researcher, and the partner team, my manager and Outlook LT became very excited about the feature. The feature started flighting just about 2 weeks ago.

New location Experience on Windows

Role: Senior Product Designer

Responsibilities: Competitive research, concept explorations, information architecture, interaction exploration

The Windows Maps app was originally built for Windows Phone. The desktop app was built later on to provide a connected experience for users. Then Microsoft devested its phone business in 2017. The Windows Maps app, along with a few other Windows applications were moved to the Windows NEXT org in 2018.

The desktop version of the Maps app's engagement stayed relatively flat over the past year given the proliferation of the smart phones and the use cases of a mapping application.

The team is tasked to reimagine how to help Windows users to have a better location experience. The new location experience focuses on bring people and friends together by integrating discovery and collective planning features in Windows Maps app and by creating a new standalone iOS and Android app.

Windows Maps App

Microsoft Fluent Design Language is a new design system the company is adopting. Windows Maps app was one of the first party apps that worked closely with the Flent team to stress tested, evolved, and co-defined the system. As the Senior UX Designer on the team, I helped to drive the direction of the new Fluent Maps exploration.

I explored the idea of allowing more business driven customization of the place card which is something Google Maps does not offer. The hypothesis is that offering the businesses to customize their own place card is a value that will help to attract more businesses to add their information to Windows Maps. This then would create a virtuous circle that would eventually attract more end users.

One of the scenarios the user often go through is to look for a few places and then compare them. There was no easy way to do that. In our exploration, we thought about the idea of bing able to pull place cards out of the app window.

Collections is a feature the team came up with that works with the new location experience we were building. The idea is that the users can co-create and share the Collections both in the Maps app and in the new location app.

Spatial UI (HoloLens)

HoloLens and Universal Windows Apps are two separate platforms Microsoft owns. They are separate from each other. Apps developed for the Windows platform can work in HoloLens but the interaction is suboptimal. This is an explorative effort with the goal to improve the experience of Windows apps in HoloLens. This video is demonstrated at the 2018 Microsoft Build conference.

I led the UX exploration and did the interaction exploration in 3DS Max and worked with a motion designer to produce the final video. I also drove the team's patent effort and at the end of the project, we filed 3 patents to protect the IP.

I started with the desktop Mail app. Broken it down into different functioning zones and explored how interaction with each functioning zone can be improved in VR/AR space. For example, the experience with the content commanding requires the user to use the 6-DOF controller to aim at very small button far away from where she stands. It makes the interaction error prone and time consuming.

Here is another motion study showing experience from first person POV. The content is summoned to a 6-DOF controller to simulate "holding" the content naturally.
